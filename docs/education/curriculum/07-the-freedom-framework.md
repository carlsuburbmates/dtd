# Module 7 - The Freedom Framework

## Module purpose

Freedom should expand only when the dog is ready for it.

This module helps the owner gradually increase room access, prevent regression, use simple household rules, and understand optional signals such as a toilet bell without turning them into a magic fix.

This is not about giving the dog strict control rules.
This is about making freedom safer, clearer, and easier to maintain.

## What the owner should be able to do after this module

By the end of Module 7, the owner should be able to:

1. Expand the dog's access gradually instead of giving the whole home at once.
2. Notice when freedom is too much too soon.
3. Prevent hidden toileting, chewing, and unsafe access.
4. Keep the safe base area available even as freedom increases.
5. Use household rules consistently.
6. Understand that a signal bell is optional, not required.
7. Avoid turning access, doors, food, or attention into confusing mixed signals.
8. Know when regression, guarding, distress, or unsafe behaviour needs trainer or vet support.

## Lesson 1 - Freedom Expands Slowly

### Scenario

Your dog has been doing well in their safe area.

They are toileting more reliably, chewing safer items, resting better, and following the household rhythm.

You wonder if it is time to let them roam the whole house.

### What the owner needs to notice

Success in one small area does not automatically mean the dog is ready for the entire home.

A bigger space means:

- more smells
- more objects
- more soft surfaces
- more hiding places
- more rooms to monitor
- more cords, shoes, bins, bags, plants, and food access
- more opportunities for hidden toileting or chewing
- more distance from the owner's supervision

Freedom is not just a reward.
Freedom is a skill the dog grows into.

### Common mistake

Expanding access too quickly.

Examples:

- giving the whole house after a few good days
- leaving doors open while the owner is distracted
- assuming one accident-free day means reliability
- removing the safe area too early
- allowing access to bedrooms, rugs, couches, laundry, garage, or children's rooms before the dog is ready
- treating regression as defiance instead of a sign the setup was too hard

### Better decision rule

Add one space at a time.

Ask:

Can my dog succeed in this new space while I am still able to supervise?

If not, the step is too big.

### What to do now

Use gradual freedom expansion.

Start with:

1. Safe base area.
2. One supervised room.
3. One short supervised trial in a second room.
4. Longer access only if toileting, chewing, and settling stay reliable.
5. Return to smaller space if accidents or unsafe chewing appear.

Before adding a room:

- puppy-proof it
- close doors
- remove hazards
- lift cords
- move shoes, bags, laundry, bins, plants, and food
- check rugs and soft surfaces
- keep the dog supervised
- keep toileting opportunities frequent
- keep the safe rest area available

Do not remove structure just because the dog is improving.

### Watch for this

Freedom may be too much if the dog:

- disappears into other rooms
- toilets where the owner cannot see
- chews furniture, cords, shoes, bedding, or rubbish
- becomes more unsettled
- cannot rest
- patrols the house
- barks at windows or outdoor sounds
- guards rooms, beds, toys, or stolen items
- becomes harder to redirect

Freedom is more appropriate if the dog:

- stays relaxed
- returns to the owner
- rests normally
- toilets in the expected place
- chews legal items
- can leave the room without panic
- can be redirected calmly
- settles after exploration

### When education is not enough

Seek trainer support if:

- the dog repeatedly toilets or chews despite a clear setup
- the dog guards rooms, beds, people, food, or stolen items
- the dog becomes distressed when access is limited
- the owner cannot safely supervise
- expansion causes barking, panic, snapping, or destructive behaviour
- the household cannot agree on boundaries

Seek vet advice if:

- toileting suddenly changes
- the dog seems unable to hold urine or stool
- the dog seems painful, unwell, restless, confused, or unusually distressed
- behaviour changes suddenly without a clear setup reason

### Trainer-readiness note

Before seeking help, record:

- which areas the dog can access
- when access was expanded
- where accidents or chewing happen
- what supervision was in place
- what the dog did before the problem
- how long the dog was unsupervised
- whether the dog had toileted recently
- what changed when access was reduced again

## Lesson 2 - Regression Is Information

### Scenario

Your dog was doing well.

Then suddenly there is a toileting accident, chewed furniture, barking at the window, stolen items, or restless pacing.

The owner thinks:

"They know better."

### What the owner needs to notice

Regression usually means something changed.

Possibilities include:

- too much freedom
- too little supervision
- fewer toilet trips
- missed rest
- new room access
- new visitors
- new sounds
- new schedule
- stress
- illness or pain
- access to tempting items
- inconsistent household responses

The dog may not be choosing to be difficult.

The setup may no longer match the dog's current ability.

### Common mistake

Responding with punishment instead of investigation.

Examples:

- yelling after an accident
- scolding over chewed items
- forcing the dog into confinement angrily
- removing all freedom without checking the trigger
- changing the whole routine again
- ignoring possible health changes

### Better decision rule

When regression appears, reduce difficulty and look for the pattern.

Ask:

What became harder for my dog recently?

Then adjust the environment before blaming the dog.

### What to do now

Use a regression reset.

For 48 hours:

1. Reduce access by one level.
2. Increase supervision.
3. Add more toilet opportunities.
4. Restore planned rest.
5. Remove tempting items.
6. Use safe chews and enrichment.
7. Track when the issue happens.
8. Contact the vet if the change is sudden, physical, or illness-related.

Do not treat smaller space as punishment.

Say:

We are making the setup easier again.

### Watch for this

Look for changes in:

- routine
- sleep
- appetite
- toileting
- visitors
- home layout
- access to new rooms
- outdoor stress
- weather
- construction noise
- household conflict
- owner work schedule
- health signs

### When education is not enough

Seek vet advice if regression is sudden or paired with:

- increased urination
- diarrhoea
- vomiting
- lethargy
- pain signs
- limping
- confusion
- appetite change
- sleep disruption
- sudden irritability
- defensive handling reaction

Seek trainer support if:

- regression continues despite reducing difficulty
- the dog becomes unsafe
- guarding, biting, or panic appears
- the owner cannot identify the pattern
- the household cannot maintain one plan

### Vet/trainer-readiness note

Record:

- what was going well before
- what changed
- when regression started
- location
- time of day
- supervision level
- rest pattern
- toileting pattern
- health signs
- what improved when freedom was reduced

## Lesson 3 - Signals and the Optional Toilet Bell

### Scenario

Your dog sometimes stands near the door, scratches, whines, barks, paces, or stares at the owner.

You wonder if they need to toilet, want attention, want outside time, or are just restless.

You have heard about using a bell for toileting.

### What the owner needs to notice

A signal is useful only if it has a clear meaning.

A toilet bell, door button, or other signal can help some dogs communicate:

"I need to go outside to toilet."

But it can also become confusing if it means:

- I want to play
- I want attention
- I want to chase birds
- I want to bark outside
- I want the door opened whenever I ask

The bell is optional.
The routine matters more than the tool.

### Common mistake

Using a bell before the toilet routine is clear.

Another mistake is opening the door every time the dog rings without checking the purpose.

This can teach the dog:

Bell means outdoor access for anything.

That may increase door-demanding, barking, or frustration.

### Better decision rule

A signal should support the routine, not replace it.

Ask:

What happens after the signal every single time?

If the answer changes constantly, the signal will become unclear.

### What to do now

Only use a bell if the household can be consistent.

If using one, keep the meaning narrow:

Bell means calm toilet trip only.

Suggested routine:

1. Place the bell near the exit used for toileting.
2. Before a planned toilet trip, prompt or wait for a light touch.
3. Immediately take the dog to the toilet area.
4. Keep the trip boring and focused.
5. Reward toileting in the correct place.
6. Return inside calmly.
7. Do not turn every bell ring into play or a walk.

If the dog rings repeatedly after just toileting:

- wait briefly
- check whether they truly need to go
- avoid turning it into a game
- review whether the dog is bored, under-rested, or seeking attention

### Watch for this

The bell may be useful if:

- the dog already understands the toilet area
- the household responds consistently
- ringing usually leads to toileting
- the dog is not becoming frantic or demanding
- accidents reduce

The bell may be causing confusion if:

- the dog rings constantly
- the dog rings to play
- the dog rings to bark outside
- the dog becomes frustrated when the door does not open
- accidents continue because routine timing is still unclear
- household members respond differently

### When education is not enough

Seek trainer support if:

- the dog cannot develop a clear toileting routine
- signalling becomes frantic or demanding
- the dog guards doorways or outdoor access
- the dog barks, scratches, or panics around exits
- the household cannot use the signal consistently

Seek vet advice if:

- toilet frequency suddenly changes
- the dog strains, cries, leaks urine, has diarrhoea, vomits, seems lethargic, or appears unable to hold urine or stool

### Trainer-readiness note

Record:

- how often the dog signals
- what signal they use
- whether toileting happens afterward
- whether the signal is used for attention or play
- who responds
- what happens after the signal
- whether accidents are improving
- whether any health signs exist

## Lesson 4 - Family Rules Make Freedom Clearer

### Scenario

One person lets the dog on the couch.

Another person removes them.

One person feeds from the table.

Another person wants begging to stop.

One person opens the door when the dog barks.

Another person wants quiet waiting.

The dog receives different rules depending on who is home.

### What the owner needs to notice

Freedom becomes confusing when the rules change.

The dog is learning from every person in the home.

Rules need to be clear for:

- rooms
- couches and beds
- doors
- food
- toys
- play
- visitors
- children
- resting places
- alone time
- outdoor access
- handling

The dog does not need harsh rules.
They need predictable ones.

### Common mistake

Expecting the dog to follow rules that humans have not agreed on.

Examples:

- some people allow jumping, others scold it
- some people open the door during barking, others ignore it
- one person allows couch access, another removes the dog angrily
- children wake the dog during rest
- visitors reward behaviour the household is trying to reduce
- stolen items become a chase game

### Better decision rule

One rule, one response, one week.

Ask:

What will everyone do the same way for the next seven days?

If the rule changes daily, the dog cannot learn it clearly.

### What to do now

Write a simple family rules template.

Start with only the most important rules.

Examples:

| Area | Rule |
| --- | --- |
| Rest area | No one disturbs the dog while resting |
| Rooms | Dog starts with supervised access only |
| Couch | Household chooses yes, no, or invitation only |
| Door | Door opens during calm pause, not barking |
| Food | No feeding from the table |
| Visitors | Visitors ignore jumping and reward calm feet |
| Stolen items | Swap, do not chase |
| Children | Children do not corner, wake, climb on, or grab the dog |

Keep the rules realistic.

A rule that no one follows is not a rule.

### Watch for this

Rules are working if:

- household arguments reduce
- dog repeats calm behaviours more often
- owner responses become quicker and clearer
- fewer behaviours are rewarded by accident
- visitors understand what to do
- the dog rests without interruption
- freedom can expand without major setbacks

Rules need adjustment if:

- they are too complicated
- children or visitors cannot follow them
- one person keeps changing the plan
- the dog becomes frustrated or unsafe
- the rule creates more conflict than clarity

### When education is not enough

Seek trainer support if:

- the household cannot agree on safe handling
- children are involved and safety is uncertain
- the dog guards food, toys, beds, people, or spaces
- the dog snaps, bites, lunges, or blocks access
- the dog becomes distressed when rules are applied
- the owner needs coaching to make a realistic household plan

Seek vet advice if:

- rule problems appear after sudden behaviour change
- the dog reacts to being touched, moved, or woken
- pain, illness, sleep disruption, or confusion may be involved

### Trainer-readiness note

Bring the family rules template to the trainer.

Record:

- which rules are unclear
- who responds differently
- which behaviours are being rewarded by accident
- whether children or visitors are involved
- whether guarding or fear is present
- whether the dog has safe rest areas
- whether the behaviour changed suddenly

## Tool 1 - Freedom Expansion Checklist

Use this before adding a new room or more unsupervised time.

### Before expanding access

- Dog is toileting reliably in current area
- Dog is chewing safe items in current area
- Dog can rest in current area
- Dog is not hiding to toilet
- Dog is not guarding rooms, beds, toys, or stolen items
- Dog has had a recent toilet opportunity
- Dog is not overtired or overstimulated
- New room is puppy-proofed
- Owner can supervise
- Safe base area remains available

### New room check

- Cords managed
- Shoes and bags removed
- Laundry removed
- Bins secured
- Food inaccessible
- Plants checked
- Small objects removed
- Doors closed where needed
- Rugs monitored
- Windows/fence triggers considered

### Trial rule

- Short trial
- Supervised
- Calm activity available
- Toilet break before trial
- Return to smaller area if problems appear
- No punishment if the trial was too hard

## Tool 3 - Optional Toilet Signal Bell Check

Use this only if the household wants a toilet signal system.

### Before using a bell

- Dog has a regular toilet routine
- Household agrees bell means toilet trip only
- Everyone uses the same door
- Everyone responds the same way
- Bell is not used for play, walks, or attention
- Dog is not already frantic around doors

### Bell routine

- Bell cue happens before planned toilet trip
- Dog goes straight to toilet area
- Trip is calm and boring
- Toileting is rewarded immediately
- Dog returns inside calmly
- Repeated non-toilet ringing is tracked, not automatically rewarded

### Track

Date/time | Bell used? | Toileted? | Wanted play/access? | Accident later? | Notes
--- | --- | --- | --- | --- | ---

Stop and reassess if:

- Bell ringing becomes constant
- Dog becomes frustrated
- Door barking increases
- Bell no longer predicts toileting
- Accidents continue
- Household responses are inconsistent

### Core source base

Module 7 should be supported by a final source-quality pass before publication because the earlier Gemini rough scan had weak or incomplete support for several topics, especially Signal Bell. That scan is retained only as an idea pool.

Use already-approved curriculum evidence from earlier modules where appropriate:

- RSPCA-style toilet-training principles: supervision, predictable toilet opportunities, rewarding correct toileting, avoiding punishment after accidents, and cleaning calmly.
- RSPCA-style puppy setup principles: safe area, rest, gradual access, hazard removal, and not using confinement as punishment.
- RSPCA and AVSAB humane-training principles: reward wanted behaviours, avoid punishment and dominance framing, use clear communication and consistent management.
- Module 5 consistency principles: dogs repeat behaviours that work; households should agree on cues, responses, and rewards.
- Module 3 and Module 4 escalation principles: sudden behaviour change, touch sensitivity, guarding, freezing, snapping, biting, or inability to recover requires vet or trainer support depending on context.

### Signal Bell caution

The Signal Bell should remain optional.

Approved wording:

A toilet bell can be used as a narrow communication tool if the household is consistent and the dog already has a clear toileting routine.

Avoid:

Every dog should use a bell.

Avoid:

A bell fixes toilet training.

Avoid:

The bell replaces supervision, timing, or toilet opportunities.

If stronger welfare or veterinary source support is not found, keep the bell as an optional tool note rather than a core curriculum requirement.

### Claims requiring careful wording

Freedom expansion

Approved wording:

Expand access gradually, one area at a time, when the dog can succeed with supervision.

Avoid:

The dog has earned full freedom permanently.

Regression

Approved wording:

Regression usually means something changed; reduce difficulty and look for the pattern.

Avoid:

The dog knows better and is being defiant.

Family rules

Approved wording:

The household should agree on clear, consistent rules so the dog receives the same information from everyone.

Avoid:

The dog is pushing boundaries or being obnoxious.

Door and access rules

Approved wording:

Access should follow calm, predictable routines.

Avoid:

Never give the dog anything for free.

Toileting signal

Approved wording:

A toilet signal should mean a calm toilet trip only.

Avoid:

Ringing the bell should always open the door for play or outdoor access.

Escalation

Approved wording:

Seek trainer or vet support when regression is sudden, unsafe, linked to guarding, linked to pain or illness, or not improving after setup changes.

Avoid:

This module treats guarding, aggression, separation distress, or medical toileting problems.
