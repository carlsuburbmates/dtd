# Prepare the First Vet Visit

## Situation

<LessonPanel eyebrow="Lesson context">
  The dog is home, the owner has food and bedding, but now needs clarity on vaccines, parasite prevention, microchipping, registration, safe early exposure, desexing questions, and what the first year may actually cost.
</LessonPanel>

## What to notice

<ChecklistBlock items={["The first vet visit is not just for vaccines. It is the first professional baseline for general health, local disease risks, behaviour-relevant health questions, and low-stress future care.", "Routine costs and unexpected costs need to be planned separately, because cost stress can quickly become a welfare issue."]} variant="checklist" />

## Common mistake

<LessonPanel eyebrow="What often makes this worse" tone="caution">
  Waiting until there is a problem, going to the vet without questions, or delaying care because the first-year costs were underestimated.
</LessonPanel>

## Decision rule

<DecisionRule label="Rule for this lesson">Book early and bring notes. Ask what this dog needs in this local area and this household, not only what the internet says puppies need.</DecisionRule>

## What to do now

<ChecklistBlock items={["Book the first vet visit and bring paperwork, food details, vaccine or microchip details, and notes about appetite, toileting, sleep, vomiting, diarrhoea, fear, handling, or sudden changes.", "Create a simple dog budget with separate routine, setup, and unexpected-cost sections before the first scare or surprise bill."]} variant="worksheet" />

## What to watch

<ChecklistBlock items={["Cost stress becomes a welfare issue when it starts delaying vet visits, prevention, or treatment.", "Speak to a vet first if the dog seems unwell, painful, lethargic, has vomiting or diarrhoea, has eaten something unsafe, or suddenly behaves differently."]} variant="checklist" />

## When to get help

<EscalationNote>
  Vet advice comes before training advice when symptoms look physical, urgent, or unsafe. Be upfront about budget limits so care can be prioritised realistically.
</EscalationNote>

## What to record

<TrainerReadiness>
  Record what changed, when it started, what the dog ate or contacted, vaccine history if known, stool quality, appetite, sleep pattern, and any fear signals during travel or handling.
</TrainerReadiness>
