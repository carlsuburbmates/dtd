# Stop Accidentally Paying for the Wrong Behaviour

## Situation

<LessonPanel eyebrow="Lesson context">
  Your dog barks for attention, jumps on visitors, paws, mouths clothing, or steals items while people look, talk, laugh, chase, push, or open the door trying to stop it.
</LessonPanel>

## What to notice

<ChecklistBlock items={["Attention can be a reward. For some dogs, eye contact, talking, touching, laughing, chasing, pushing, or opening access is enough to make the behaviour happen again.", "The dog is not being spiteful if the behaviour keeps working."]} variant="checklist" />

## Common mistake

<LessonPanel eyebrow="What often makes this worse" tone="caution">
  Reacting strongly to behaviour the owner wants to reduce and accidentally rewarding it with attention, access, or movement.
</LessonPanel>

## Decision rule

<DecisionRule label="Rule for this lesson">Reward the replacement, not the chaos. Ask what the dog should do instead, then reward that instead of the old behaviour.</DecisionRule>

## What to do now

<ChecklistBlock items={["Choose one repeating behaviour and write down what you will stop rewarding, and what you will reward instead.", "Set the dog up to succeed with management such as a lead, baby gate, safe room, food scatter, mat, calm greeting plan, fewer people, or more distance when the situation is too hard.", "Do not rely on willpower in a hard situation; reduce rehearsal while the replacement becomes easier to reward."]} variant="worksheet" />

## What to watch

<ChecklistBlock items={["The unwanted behaviour may briefly increase when it stops working. That does not automatically mean the plan is wrong.", "Track whether the replacement behaviour is staying easy and whether the household is still accidentally rewarding the old behaviour."]} variant="checklist" />

## When to get help

<EscalationNote>
  Seek support if the behaviour is intense or unsafe, the dog becomes frustrated or aggressive, rehearsal cannot be prevented, children, visitors, or other animals are at risk, the behaviour is linked to fear, panic, guarding, or distress, or the household cannot apply the same response.
</EscalationNote>

## What to record

<TrainerReadiness>
  Tell a trainer what the dog gets from the behaviour, whether that is attention, food, play, distance, access, escape, or movement, what replacement you tried, whether the behaviour changed, how the household responded, and whether safety concerns are present.
</TrainerReadiness>
