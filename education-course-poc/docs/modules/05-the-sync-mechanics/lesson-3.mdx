# Calm Routines Before Big Feelings

## Situation

<LessonPanel eyebrow="Lesson context">
  The dog rushes the food bowl, pushes through doors, jumps at greetings, barks when ignored, grabs the lead before walks, or becomes frantic before play, and the owner feels like the dog has no manners.
</LessonPanel>

## What to notice

<ChecklistBlock items={["Many manners problems are routine problems. The dog is learning what starts food, doors, play, attention, movement, and waiting every day.", "If every exciting event begins with chaos, the dog may rehearse chaos every day."]} variant="checklist" />

## Common mistake

<LessonPanel eyebrow="What often makes this worse" tone="caution">
  Waiting until the dog is already overexcited, then asking for too much too late.
</LessonPanel>

## Decision rule

<DecisionRule label="Rule for this lesson">Build the routine before the dog tips over. Start with the calmest version of the moment the dog can manage.</DecisionRule>

## What to do now

<ChecklistBlock items={["Pick one daily routine and use the same pattern each time: food, door, greeting, or attention.", "For food, prepare calmly, wait for one tiny calm moment, lower the bowl, and pause if the dog rushes.", "For doors, wait for a quiet pause, open slightly, close calmly if the dog surges, and reward calm waiting by opening again.", "For greetings or attention, keep the setup low-key, reward four paws down or a quiet pause, and use gates, distance, or lower intensity if needed."]} variant="worksheet" />

## What to watch

<ChecklistBlock items={["Track whether the dog becomes calmer when the routine becomes predictable: shorter excitement bursts, quicker recovery, more waiting, less jumping, less barking, more checking in, and easier settling after the event.", "Notice which routine still tips over first and whether the step needs to be made easier."]} variant="checklist" />

## When to get help

<EscalationNote>
  Seek support if the dog cannot calm even with easier routines, arousal escalates into biting, snapping, guarding, or lunging, the dog becomes distressed when access is delayed, doors, food, greetings, or visitors cannot be managed safely, or the dog appears frantic, unwell, or unable to rest.
</EscalationNote>

## What to record

<TrainerReadiness>
  Record the routine chosen, what happens before excitement starts, what the dog does, what the owner does, what reward is used, what makes it easier, what makes it worse, and whether the dog recovers afterward.
</TrainerReadiness>
